Explainer

Special needs trusts (SNTs)

What is a special needs trust?

A special needs trust, or SNT, is a legal tool that allows families to set aside money or property to support a person with a disability without putting their eligibility for essential benefits, like Medicaid or Supplemental Security Income (SSI), at risk. These programs have strict rules about how much money someone can have, and even a modest inheritance or gift can push someone over the limit and cause them to lose coverage. A Special Needs Trust helps prevent that.

First-party trust

Best when there's a lawsuit, back pay, or inheritance in the person's name. The person with a disability puts in the money; Medicaid may be paid back first.

Third-party trust

Best when family is doing long-term planning. Someone else (like a parent) puts in the money; leftover funds go to other family members as stated in the trust.

Pooled trust

Best when family wants a lower-cost option or help managing funds. Managed by a nonprofit; leftover funds depend on the nonprofit's rules.

How do special needs trusts work?

Creating a Special Needs Trust usually requires help from an attorney who specializes in disability or elder law. Once established, it's managed by a trustee — a parent, sibling, professional fiduciary, bank, or nonprofit organization. Trustees are responsible for keeping good records and reporting spending to programs like SSI or Medicaid.

Section 8 and Housing Choice Vouchers

What is a Housing Choice Voucher?

A Housing Choice Voucher, often called Section 8, helps low-income households — including people with disabilities — pay for housing in the private market. The voucher covers a portion of rent directly to the landlord, and the household pays the rest, typically around 30 percent of their income.

Who administers it

Local Public Housing Authorities (PHAs) manage waitlists and issue vouchers in each county or region.

What to know

Waitlists can be long — often years. Applying early and to multiple PHAs can help.

How it works with disability benefits

Housing vouchers are based on household income, so families should understand how SSI, SSDI, or other benefits are counted when a Public Housing Authority determines eligibility and rent share.

Guardianship vs. supported decision-making

What happens at age 18?

In Florida, a person is considered a legal adult at 18, regardless of disability, and gains the right to make their own decisions about medical care, education, money, and where to live — unless a court says otherwise. Families of a person with I/DD often need to decide whether guardianship, supported decision-making, or another option best fits their loved one's needs.

Guardianship

A court gives a guardian legal authority to make some or all decisions for the person. This removes some or all of the person's own decision-making rights, so Florida courts favor less restrictive options first.

Supported decision-making

The person keeps their legal right to make their own decisions, with trusted supporters helping them understand options and communicate choices. No court process is required.

Power of attorney / healthcare surrogate

The person voluntarily authorizes someone else to act on their behalf in specific areas, while keeping their own decision-making rights intact.

How to decide

Florida law requires courts to consider less-restrictive alternatives to guardianship first. Many families start with a supported decision-making agreement and only pursue guardianship if it proves insufficient for their loved one's safety and well-being.

What does a support coordinator do?

What is a support coordinator?

A support coordinator (sometimes called a case manager) helps individuals with I/DD and their families identify needs, understand available services, and access the Medicaid waiver supports they're eligible for. They act as a guide through a system that can otherwise feel impossible to navigate alone.

What they help with

Developing a support plan, connecting to waiver providers, coordinating between agencies, and advocating on the family's behalf.

How to get one

Support coordinators are typically assigned once a person is enrolled in a Medicaid waiver, such as Florida's iBudget Waiver.

What they don't do

A support coordinator doesn't provide direct services themselves — they connect you to providers who do.

Why this relationship matters

Families consistently report that a good support coordinator relationship is one of the biggest factors in successfully accessing services over the long term — this is often the single most important connection point in the entire system.

Self-advocacy: what it is and why it matters

What is self-advocacy?

Self-advocacy means a person with a disability speaking up for themselves — expressing their own needs, preferences, and rights, and making their own decisions whenever possible. Self-advocacy organizations, often led by people with I/DD themselves, provide training, peer support, and community to help build these skills.

Why it matters

Self-advocacy skills support more independence, better outcomes in supported decision-making, and stronger self-esteem.

How families can help

Encourage choice-making from an early age, connect your loved one to self-advocacy groups, and practice supported decision-making at home.

Getting connected

Many regions have local self-advocacy chapters that meet regularly, often connected to statewide or national self-advocacy networks.

Early signs and the evaluation process

What are early signs of a developmental delay?

Every child develops at their own pace, but consistent delays in speech, motor skills, social interaction, or behavior compared to developmental milestones can be an early sign. Pediatricians routinely screen for these markers at well-child visits.

Who can evaluate

Pediatricians, Early Steps (birth-3), or a school district's Child Find program (age 3+) can conduct or refer for a formal evaluation.

What to expect

A team of specialists assesses development across several areas; results determine eligibility for early intervention or special education services.

After the evaluation

If your child qualifies, an Individualized Family Support Plan (IFSP) for children under 3, or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) for school-age children, is developed to guide services and supports.

Choosing early intervention services (birth to 3)

Tips for early intervention
  • Earlier connection to services is linked to stronger long-term developmental outcomes.
  • Early Steps services in Florida are provided at no cost to families, regardless of income.
  • Ask how services are delivered — home-based, center-based, or a mix of both.

IEP vs. 504 plan

Understand the difference between the two most common school-based support plans.

Tips for the process
  • Both plans are free and are your child's legal right if they qualify — schools cannot charge for evaluations or services.
  • You can request an evaluation for either plan in writing at any time.
  • You're an equal member of the IEP team — your input and observations matter as much as the school's.
Explainer

Transitioning from school-based to adult services

Why transition planning matters

Special education services and entitlements end when a student graduates or ages out (typically at 22 in Florida). Adult disability services work differently — they are not automatic and often require separate applications, evaluations, and waitlists. Starting the planning process early helps avoid gaps in support.

When to start

Transition planning legally must begin by age 16, but many families and schools start earlier, around age 14.

What's involved

The IEP team develops a transition plan covering education, employment, and independent living goals, in coordination with the student and family.

Connecting to adult services

Families should connect with Vocational Rehabilitation and APD (for waiver services) well before graduation, since eligibility, applications, and waitlists for adult services can take significant time.

Explainer

Vocational Rehabilitation services

What is Vocational Rehabilitation (VR)?

Florida's Division of Vocational Rehabilitation helps people with disabilities prepare for, find, and keep employment. Services can include career counseling, job skills training, job placement assistance, and workplace accommodations.

Who's eligible

Individuals with a documented disability that creates a barrier to employment, who need VR services to work.

How to apply

Applications are submitted directly to a local VR office; an eligibility determination and individualized plan for employment follow.

Working alongside other supports

VR services can work alongside waiver-funded supported employment services, so it helps to coordinate with your support coordinator if you have one.

Understanding Medicaid Managed Care plans

What is Medicaid Managed Care?

In Florida, most Medicaid recipients get their care through a Medicaid Managed Care plan — a private health plan contracted by the state to coordinate and pay for services, rather than fee-for-service Medicaid.

What it covers

Doctor visits, hospital care, prescriptions, and often behavioral health services, depending on the plan.

Choosing a plan

Families typically choose from several available plans in their region and can compare provider networks and covered services.

How it works with waiver services

Medicaid Managed Care covers standard medical care, while iBudget Waiver services cover additional disability-specific supports — most people with I/DD who are on the waiver use both together.

Tips for coordinating coverage
  • Many families use Medicaid and private insurance together — Medicaid can act as secondary coverage.
  • The iBudget Waiver doesn't replace medical insurance — it funds additional disability-specific supports.
  • Ask your plan about care coordination if your loved one sees multiple specialists.
